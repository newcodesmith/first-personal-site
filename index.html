LEARN TO LEARN

Learn to Learn is a process consisting of three parts to increase the effectiveness at which one learns. These three parts are as follows.
- Clearing the stage
- Clarifying the what and the why of what you want to learn
- Prioritize process over product.
Below you will find some examples of this process.

Learn to Learn Example #1

What do I want to learn?
- How to make shapes using HTML and CSS.

Setting the stage.
- Went to the bathroom.
- Got a glass of water.
- Moved to quite room.
- Cleared other open windows on computer.
- Silenced phone.

Play
- Today in class we were asked to recreate a poster using HTML and CSS. There were shapes in the poster that I did not have experience with making and was clearing going to need to figure out how to make these shapes. I decided to use my good old friend google to look for the answer. I did see a few options in my search and decided on one to make a triangle for the poster using CSS. The process was much simpler than I thought it would be. When I started with the stock settings I realized that I was going to need to make some adjustments. Once messing with the settings, a little bit, I could see how the settings affected the shape and was able to quickly size it to my specific specifications.

Reflection
- I was able to complete the task and it helped having my area clean and clear and my coffee so I did not get sleepy. When switching between windows I did not get distracted by other apps running making the switch more efficient. I hope to check out more of the example shapes in the article that I found and apply them to other future projects.

Meetup #1 Reflection

The name and date of the meetup
- Thursday, December 7, 2017
- Progressive Web Apps - Fitter, happier, more-productive UIs

The name and job information of at least 2 people you met
- Nick Stevens – User Experience Lead
- David Zuluaga – Developer
- Lena J – Developer – Just finish school at Fullstack

A 1-2 paragraph description of what you learned at the meetup
- The meetup was hosted by Kenzan which is a software engineering/professional services company that provides customized end-to-end solutions. The discussion focused on Progressive Web Apps. The average phone user only uses abut 3 of the apps they install on their phone. Most everything else is access via the browser. With Progressive Web App’s, developers are able to design a user experience that feels native and runs much faster as it caches some of the information on the device after the first use. This is valuable as most of the world only has access to 2G internet speeds. They did go over some code but I could only understand a little bit of it. I do feel that Progressive Web Apps are the future and I am excited to learn more about them.

- I don’t really have any current questions. They did provide us with an exercise that I would like to try and do once I learn more JavaScript. I am sure I will come up with additional questions after working that.
